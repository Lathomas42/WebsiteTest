Creative Title Here

Logan Thomas

Throughout this semester, this course has pushed me to become a better writer in mathematics and physics writing. I have explored different writing styles throughout the semester that allowed me to find my strengths and weaknesses as a writer, offering the opportunity to improve myself in those areas of weakness. For a person of my profession, the three key learning goals from this course are to learn how to: Formulate and articulate a stance through and in my writing, write to learn and to communicate what I learn, and reflecting on my writing process and self-assess as a writer.

The first goal, to formulate and articulate a stance through and in my writing, is important to me because with any sort of data analysis I do, I will need to provide in depth analysis of whatever results I find. This analysis requires me to formulate and articulate a stance. This course has helped me in this because this formulation and articulation of a stance has been central to many of the assignments thus far.

I think that the best example of my writing that expresses this first goal of learning in this course is my Op-ed on Mathematics education. In that essay, I began by expressing the need for a better education in mathematics, followed by an opinionated review of the reason to teach math. I then provide other sources saying similar things to me or backing my claim. Then, I address a few key problems people have with math, followed by why those problems are mislead and offer solutions to fix these problems. Through this essay, I feel my argument was well founded with outside sources and well articulated points such as, after citing a study done about different ways to teach children math I said, “It is for this reason that math problems are not necessarily about practicality, and never have been. Math is known and always has been known for giving useless, impractical problems to see how students approach a problem they don't already know how to solve.”(Math Op-Ed) This point follows evidence and addresses a counterpoint to my argument, both strengthening my claim and weakening the opposing claim.

The second main goal of the course, to write to learn and communicate what I learn, is a very important skill to have because it allows me to write my way through a problem. This skill in combination with the first skill allows me to write a paper without a specific argument in mind and finish the paper with a well formed articulate argument based on the evidence provided in the paper.

I showed my learning of this goal in my most recent essay reviewing modern methods of modularity optimization. I wrote this essay because it is a problem I was facing at work, choosing which of the key modularity optimization methods to select. I came into the essay with no preference and I have used a few of the methods with varied results. After writing the essay I had formed a strong preference for the Louvain method. I began the essay with unbiased definitions and introductions to all the methods reviewed. The second part of the essay where the table was included and analysis of the results of the table show that the Louvain method was clearly better than the other methods allowed me to learn with the reader that that method was better than others. This essay allowed me to begin with no opinion on a topic and end with a well founded opinion.

The third main goal of the course for me was to be able to reflect on my writing process and self-assess as a writer. This is an invaluable skill to learn as it allows me to always improve. Acknowledging that I am not a great writer and being able to asses where in my writing I need work is vital in improving myself and my arguments. This is not necessarily profession specific as it pertains to many aspects of life.

The combination of these three goals from this course I feel can further me in my profession whether it be in math, physics or data science. Formulating a stance through my writing is a vital skill in presenting any argument through analysis and presentation of evidence, being able to learn through writing is an invaluable way to get myself out of any pickle where I am unsure of the correct opinion in an argument. Finally, I am continuing to learn and improve my writing by assessing myself along the way attempting to identify my strengths and weaknesses.

In this example I used Matplotlib's bar plot function to show a large dataset, all Olympic athletes. Specifically showing how many medals were won in each age group.

In the bar graph, the different colors represent the breakdown of golds,silvers, and bronzes inside the total amount of medals. This was done by using the fact that in matplotlib, the first thing you call to be plotted will be under everything else. So I plotted all medals won as a gold bar plot, then all bronze and silver medals as a silver plot, then all bronze medals as a bronze plot.

The library Numpy was also used in this example. Numpy is a widely used python library that allows very fast calculations using numpy-arrays. Whenever you are working with a large data set, use numpy to speed things up.

The data used for this example is a free data-set from Sports Reference who has a large (8618 athlete) dataset for the history of the olympics.

This data was already formatted in a .xlsx format though I switched it to a .csv type to be easily readable in python. The csv was formatted in columns like so:

Athlete,Age,Country,Year,Closing Ceremony Date,Sport,Gold Medals,Silver Medals,Bronze Medals,Total Medals

Although this was way too much information for what I wanted to do so I only payed attention to the Age and the Medals Columns, ignoring 5 columns.

There are thousands of free data-sets on-line, this is a great example of one of them but many more can be found in places like data.gov. A word of warning with free data-sets, they often have too much information, such as this case, where we ignored many of the columns, The unnecessary information should be thrown away at the start to reduce memory used.

In this example I used PyQtGraph's ScatterPlotItem() Class which behaves as a normal Scatter Plot should. The Plus side for PyQtGraph is that it is fast and deals very well with large Datasets. For this reason I used two large datasets (described in the data tab). Making 4 scatter-plots with the datasets, each scatter plot comparing 2 variables (x and y axis) and a third using the color of the nodes.

The left column of scatter plots are Red Wine comparisons and the Right Column are white wine comparisons. The top plots are pH vs Alcohol content and the bottom plots are Residual sugar vs Density. All of the nodes are colored according to their quality rating, the scale can be seen in the bottom right graph.

The data used in this plot are large data-sets downloaded from UCI's machine learning database, they are two datasets, preformatted in csv form. One set is red wine(~1600 entries), one set is white wine(~4900 entries). Both sets are similarly formated into columns according to :

"fixed acidity";"volatile acidity";"citric acid";"residual sugar";"chlorides";"free sulfur dioxide";"total sulfur dioxide";"density";"pH";"sulphates";"alcohol";"quality"

UCI is another place on the internet to get great sets of data for free. This particular set allowed me to compare different qualities of red and white wine to the rating to look at trends. Note that in this example, there were many different comparisons I could have made between qualities. The top comparison seems to be a decent comparison because we see a clear trend in rating as we go to higher pH/alchohol content. The bottom plots seem to be poor comparisons because there is no real trend going on.

Sometimes when you have large sets of data with many different qualities it requires you to hunt around and try some different comparisons or plot types before you get one that has a significant trend.
